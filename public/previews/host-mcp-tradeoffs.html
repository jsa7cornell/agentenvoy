Host-side MCP — three tradeoff calls
Each tab is one decision. Side-by-side mockups; same scenario, three product behaviors. Author's lean is highlighted but not pre-decided.
When the host's own Claude posts in a deal-room, what does Envoy do?
Envoy is the host's in-product delegate. The host's own Claude (via host-MCP) is also acting on their behalf. Two delegates representing the same principal in the same room. The question is how they coexist.
Scenario
John (to his Claude, in a separate Claude session): "Tell Bryan I can do Tuesday at 3pm if that works for him."
— but: John has a calendar block at 2:30pm Tuesday that Envoy knows about, and Claude doesn't.
A Envoy replies in-thread
Like guest-side `post_message` — Envoy generates a follow-up turn after Claude posts.
Bryan ↔ John (deal-room)
Bryan· 2:14pm
What time works for you next week?
via Claude· John
Tuesday at 3pm work?
Envoy· John's assistant
Quick note — John has something at 2:30 that day. 3pm is tight if it runs over. Want me to also offer 4pm?
Bryan's view: Two of John's "voices" disagree mid-thread. He has to pick one — and now Envoy second-guessed Claude in front of him. Classic two-cooks problem.
What it gives you: Safety net. Envoy catches Claude's blind spots in front of the guest.
— Two host-side voices disagreeing publicly. Reads as confused, not careful.
— Envoy is talking to its own teammate (host's Claude) while Bryan watches. Weird.
B Envoy silent
Claude posts. Envoy doesn't reply. Doesn't validate either.
Bryan ↔ John (deal-room)
Bryan· 2:14pm
What time works for you next week?
via Claude· John
Tuesday at 3pm work?
— Envoy stays out —
Failure mode: Bryan accepts. The booking lands. Two days later John realizes the 2:30 block conflicts. Now he's awkwardly rescheduling, and his Claude looks like the one that screwed up.
What it gives you: Cleanest deal-room. Single host voice per turn. Predictable.
+ One delegate speaks. Bryan never sees the host arguing with itself.
— No safety net. Claude's blind spots ship straight to the guest.
C Envoy silent to guest, validates host-side
Author's new lean — splits "voice" from "guardrail".
Bryan ↔ John (deal-room)
Bryan· 2:14pm
What time works for you next week?
via Claude· John
Tuesday at 3pm work?
— Envoy stays out of the deal-room —
— but in John's separate Claude session, BEFORE the post lands —
⚠Heads up before this lands
Claude wants to send: "Tuesday at 3pm work?"
You have a block at Tue 2:30pm. 3pm is 30 min after; if the prior runs over, this one's tight.
What this is: Envoy still does the safety-check, but it talks to you, not to Bryan. The deal-room stays clean; you stay informed. The host-side Claude flow has a guardrail without making your delegate look indecisive in public.
What it gives you: Both. Single host voice in the room AND a real guardrail.
+ Bryan sees one clean voice. You see Envoy's check before anything lands.
+ Matches "Envoy as your delegate" framing — Envoy talks to you, not over you.
— More to build (validation pipe + heads-up surface in the host Claude session).
Author's lean Option C B was the lean before this mockup; thinking through it concretely surfaced the third option. C respects the rule that Envoy is the host's delegate — a delegate that talks to the host (not over the host's other delegate, in front of the guest) is the right shape. The cost is real (more to build), but smaller than the cost of either two voices arguing publicly (A) or no safety net (B).
When Claude tries to mint a link to someone, when does AgentEnvoy ask you first?
Minting a link with an email triggers outreach. The product question: which emails does the host's Claude need explicit ack for?
Scenario
John says to his Claude, three times: "Find a time with Bryan — bryan@example.com." (Bryan: 2 prior meetings via AgentEnvoy.)
"Find a time with Sarah — sarah@example.com." (Sarah: 1 prior meeting.)
"Find a time with this LinkedIn person — pulse@cold-outreach-saas.com." (Never met.)
A Auto-learn from session history
User-level salt; AgentEnvoy infers "known" from past sessions.
10:01
→ create_link(bryan@example.com)
silently approved (in known-set)
10:02
→ create_link(sarah@example.com)
silently approved (in known-set)
10:03
→ create_link(pulse@cold-outreach-saas.com)
consent_required — NEW: Claude wants to reach out to pulse@cold-outreach-saas.com
What John sees: Two silent successes, then one consent banner for the cold outreach. Smooth for known recipients.
What John doesn't see: AgentEnvoy quietly hash-checked Bryan and Sarah against a per-user salted set of every email John has ever interacted with. The "known set" is invisible. If a recipient he met once a year ago gets auto-approved, John can't tell that's why.
What it gives you: Zero-friction repeat outreach.
+ Smoothest UX for the common case.
— Adds a User-level email-hash-set: a privacy surface that doesn't exist today.
— Carve-out from the per-link-salt invariant in MCP SPEC §4.
— Implicit. John has no surface where he can audit "who is Claude allowed to reach out to silently?"
B Always ask first time, allowlist host grows
Reviewer's lean. First touch always asks; explicit allowlist.
10:01
→ create_link(bryan@example.com)
consent_required — Claude wants to reach out to bryan@example.com
John clicks "Approve and remember" → bryan added to allowlist
10:02
→ create_link(sarah@example.com)
consent_required — Claude wants to reach out to sarah@example.com
John approves → sarah added.
10:03
→ create_link(pulse@cold-outreach-saas.com)
consent_required — Claude wants to reach out to pulse@cold-outreach-saas.com
John dismisses (no approve) → not added.
Settings → Connectors → Allowlist (after approve)
bryan@example.comadded 10:01×
sarah@example.comadded 10:02×
What John sees: Three banners. One click each (or dismiss). After that, future Claude calls to Bryan and Sarah are silent. The list is John's, visible, editable.
What it gives you: Explicit control. No invisible auto-learning.
+ No new privacy surface. No hash-set of who you've met.
+ Allowlist is auditable. John can revoke "stop letting Claude reach out to that person" anytime.
— First touch with anyone is a banner. Friction for the first session.
C Always ask, BUT one banner with smart suggestions
Hybrid — first-touch friction visible, but bundled and 1-click.
10:01
→ create_link × 3 (bryan, sarah, pulse@…)
🤖Claude wants to reach out to 3 people
For each, AgentEnvoy will mint a link and send the recipient a notification.
Recent guests (you've met before)
✓ Bryan bryan@example.com · 2 prior meetings
✓ Sarah sarah@example.com · 1 prior
New recipient
pulse@cold-outreach-saas.com never met
☑ Add approved recipients to allowlist (silent next time)
What John sees: One banner, three rows. Known guests pre-checked (visibility) but easy 1-click approve. Cold outreach unchecked by default, name/domain visible. Total decision time: ~5 seconds.
What it gives you: A's smoothness for known + B's safety/transparency for cold.
+ John can see WHO Claude is reaching out to before saying yes.
+ "Recent guests" row uses session history as suggestion not as silent override. Same data, different surface.
+ Allowlist still grows from approves; future Claude calls to allowlisted people are silent.
— Slightly more UI to design (the bundled multi-recipient banner shape).
Author's lean Option C B was my new lean from the review. But B treats single-recipient and multi-recipient cases the same (banner per call), which is the wrong shape when Claude is doing batch outreach. C uses session history as a UX suggestion (visible, pre-checked) rather than a silent override — gives A's smoothness with B's transparency. The privacy surface from A vanishes because nothing is auto-approved without a click.
First time someone connects Claude to AgentEnvoy, what do they feel?
Brand-new PAT. User adds it as a custom connector in Claude. They open Claude and ask the simplest possible question:
Scenario
John, in Claude, having just minted his first PAT: "What's on my AgentEnvoy?"
— Claude needs three reads to answer: list_my_links, list_my_sessions, get_my_availability.
A Silent reads (auto_approve_read default)
Reads fire silently. User sees Claude's answer.
0:00
User asks "What's on my AgentEnvoy?"
0:01
→ list_my_links
silent
0:01
→ list_my_sessions
silent
0:02
→ get_my_availability
silent
0:03
Claude
"You have 5 active links and 2 sessions in progress. Sarah's been waiting on confirmation since Tuesday; Mike just sent his first response."
What John feels: "Wow that worked." Smooth.
What he didn't see: Three silent reads of his calendar, links, and active deal-rooms. He doesn't know that yet. He'll find out (correctly) when destructive actions trigger banners — but reads fired with zero visibility.
Vibe: "It just works."
+ Friction-light first touch. The agent feels capable.
— First-time user has no exposure to the consent model. They learn it by surprise (when a write triggers a banner).
— Privacy-conscious users may feel the data left their control without notice.
B Ask everything (always_ask default)
Every call is a banner. Relax via checkbox after first session.
0:00
User asks "What's on my AgentEnvoy?"
0:01
Claude wants to: read your links → Approve / Deny
0:05
Claude wants to: read your active sessions → Approve / Deny
0:09
Claude wants to: read your calendar availability → Approve / Deny
0:13
Claude
"You have 5 active links and 2 sessions in progress…"
0:14
⚙ "You approved 3 reads. ☑ Trust this token to read silently next time?"
What John feels: Three banners in a row before getting an answer. Possibly: "OK I get it, stop asking." Possibly: "Why is this so paranoid."
What's true: John has now SEEN the consent surface. He knows reads can fire silently if he wants — because he just turned them silent on purpose.
Vibe: "Pay attention before it does anything."
+ Highest trust: user explicitly opted into silent reads.
+ User now knows the model exists and can navigate it.
— Three banners before the first answer. Some users revoke the token in frustration.
— Slow first impression of "agent doing work for me."
C Silent + first-time non-blocking toasts
Reads fire silently AND a discreet toast slides in for the first occurrence of each.

— toasts fade out auto-dismissing —
0:09
Second time the same tool fires (today or ever): no toast. Quiet.
What John feels: Three small slide-ins as Claude works, then they fade. Claude answers fast. He saw the model, didn't have to act on it.
What's true: Per-tool one-time exposure. Future reads with that token are quiet (no toast for repeated tool calls). The ⚙ link in each toast opens Settings → Connectors so anyone curious can dig in.
Vibe: "Fast, but visible."
+ Best of both: silent execution, visible model.
+ Toasts auto-dismiss; nothing blocks Claude.
+ Power user can disable toasts in settings; default-on is the right safety nudge.
— Adds a notification component (toast queue) to the host UI.
— Tier 1 notification bell (WISHLIST — `Notification` table coming) might absorb this; need to decide whether toast is independent or rides on that surface.
Author's lean Option C A was my original lean. Walking through the timelines: A trades transparency for smoothness, B trades smoothness for transparency. C is the obvious resolution if we're willing to add a toast component — and the WISHLIST Tier-1 Notification bell is already coming, so the surface partially exists. C makes the consent model self-revealing without a single blocking step.
